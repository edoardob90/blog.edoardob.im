<p><em>This review may contain spoilers.</em></p>

<p>A second viewing helped me to fit correctly together the jigsaw that Nolan, as is his inclination, always serves to his audience. Even though I found it to be more enjoyable the second time around – I realized the significance of the color vs. b/n sequences – my overall impression was virtually unchanged. I would split it into two parts: technique and plot.</p><p>Without any doubt, I rate the first section 4.5/5. It's an outstanding movie, in my opinion, and it demonstrates the mastery of the cinematic medium that Nolan – and his group of brilliant collaborators – have always (or nearly always) been capable of, despite several ups and downs. Even on a second viewing, the length is not an issue. I'd go see it again just to enjoy the very high technical quality of this cinematic product.</p><p>The second part is about the plot itself, the most troublesome, and I can only give it 3/5. The primary flaw, in my opinion, is the lack of inner, emotional exploration in many of the characters, especially the main character. Many moral dilemmas are alluded to, but hardly any are addressed from his perspective or that of some of his colleagues who had an unambiguous awareness of them. These existential issues do not appear to have tormented anyone, beginning with Oppenheimer, who had a troubled mind from an early age. It's difficult to believe that someone with his intellectual breadth didn't think extensively about any of these issues. His post-1945 bombing visions are the only insights into these inner conflicts. There is also no mention of Oppenheimer having to use all of his persuasive power (well known to anyone who knew him) to sway some of his colleagues who remained skeptical to the end – Enrico Fermi, for example, to name a character who is unfairly given very little space without understanding why. I'd go so far as to suggest that Fermi's relevance in the context of this story is significantly higher than Einstein's, who had at least ten net minutes of screen time. It's not enough to reply "But this is Oppenheimer's bio, not Fermi's," because Nolan kept us sitting for three hours! Did he truly lack time?</p><p>It's undoubtedly a film you should view in a theater (preferably a decent one, not a tiny room). I'd venture to say it's one of Nolan's top three, but I'm not sure I believe that ranking because I'd have to rewatch several of his less recent works.</p>
